\textbf{Context:} The growing adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), has intensified concerns regarding transparency, fairness, and accountability, especially in light of documented incidents of algorithmic bias, discriminatory automated decisions, and limited auditability. Although ethical guidelines and regulatory frameworks (e.g., UNESCO, OECD, EU AI Act) provide high-level principles, the literature reveals that the technical operationalization of these principles remains immature. A critical gap has been identified in the \textit{Testing} phase of the Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC), characterized by dependence on manual audits and scarcity of automated tools capable of detecting ethical violations in deployed AI systems.
\textbf{Objective:} This dissertation aims to develop and evaluate a set of automated \textit{fuzzing engines} capable of detecting ethical risks related to \textit{Transparency}, \textit{Fairness}, and \textit{Accountability} in deployed AI models, providing tools that can be integrated into real-world Software Engineering workflows. The focus on testing is justified by the need for model-agnostic, \textit{black-box} auditing mechanisms capable of validating system behavior independently of the training data used.
\textbf{Method:} The research follows a \textit{Design Science Research} (DSR) approach. A Systematic Mapping Study (2021--2025) was conducted to identify the most prevalent ethical principles and existing technical gaps. Ethical risks were then specified, mapped to suitable fuzzing techniques, and implemented in three modular \textit{fuzzing engines} employing metamorphic testing relations and adversarial prompt injection techniques. Finally, the artifacts will be empirically evaluated through \textit{black-box} fuzzing campaigns applied to commercial and open-source models, generating adversarial test cases per risk and recording detection rates, false positives, and validation against literature metrics (statistical disparity, semantic inconsistency).
\textbf{Results:} The mapping revealed \textit{Fairness}, \textit{Accountability}, and \textit{Transparency} as the most prominent principles in the literature and those with the most concrete operationalization approaches. Key gaps include the lack of automated, integrable tools and limited coverage of ethical testing. The proposed engines will provide automated test-case generation, oracles based on literature metrics (such as disparity rates and semantic robustness) per principle, and metrics for ethical compliance. Of the 11 risks identified in the taxonomy developed by participants of the project to which this research is part, 6 proved fully verifiable through automation, while 5 require complementary human evaluation due to dependence on social context.
\textbf{Conclusion:} The proposed artifacts are expected to reduce the gap between abstract ethical principles and practical verification mechanisms, enabling systematic detection of ethical failures in AI models and contributing to more robust governance, quality assurance, and auditing practices within Software Engineering. Moreover, this work advances the state of the art by offering a modular and automated approach for ethical testing, directly addressing gaps identified in the mapping study.
